GRE Preparation Resources

Covering Quantitative, Verbal, and Analytical Writing — with what to use and when.

Start here: Take the free official ETS PowerPrep test in week 1 before studying anything. Your diagnostic score tells you exactly where to focus. Skipping this wastes months.
Official ETS Resources — Always Use These
ETS PowerPrep Online
Free
2 full-length official GRE practice tests with real retired questions. Use one for diagnosis (Month 1), one for final dress rehearsal (Month 10).
When: Week 1 + Month 10
ets.org → PowerPrep

Book ~$35
The official ETS prep book with real questions and explanations. The Quant and Verbal review sections are foundational. Buy the latest edition only.
When: Months 1–6, as your primary reference
Amazon → Official GRE Guide

Book ~$20
150 real Verbal questions from ETS with explanations. Far more valuable than third-party verbal prep.

ets.org → Practice Books

150 real Quant questions with detailed explanations. Essential for understanding how ETS tests math — the logic and traps are distinct from standard problems.
When: Months 2–7 for quant deep-dive
ets.org → Practice Books

Notes System

One system for everything — GRE, English, work. No scattered notebooks.

The principle: Notes are only useful if you review them. A simple system you use beats an elaborate one you abandon. Pick ONE tool.
Recommended Tools — Pick One
📓 Notion — Best Overall
Free, syncs across all devices. Create a "Study Hub" with pages for GRE Vocab, GRE Mistakes, English Notes, and Work Learning.
Best for: People who want structure and cross-device access.
notion.so (free)
🃏 Anki — Best for Memorization
Spaced-repetition flashcard app. Scientifically proven to be the most efficient memorization method. Use for GRE vocab and formulas.
Best for: Vocabulary, formulas, anything needing long-term memorization.
apps.ankiweb.net (free)
📱 Apple Notes / Google Keep
Zero friction — always there, always fast. Use one note per topic and update immediately when you learn something. Simplicity wins.
Best for: People who hate app-switching.
📔 Physical Notebook (A5)
One A5 notebook with index tabs: GRE Quant, GRE Verbal, English, Work. Writing by hand improves retention.
Best for: Deep work sessions and people who retain info better writing by hand.
What to Actually Note Down
📐 GRE Quant — Error Log
Every wrong question: write (1) question type, (2) what you thought, (3) what was correct, (4) the rule you missed. Review weekly — you'll see patterns.
📖 GRE Verbal — Vocabulary Cards
New word: write the word, definition, root, and an example sentence you write yourself. Use Anki to review. Target: 10 new words/day.
✍️ AWA — Essay Feedback Log
After every essay: note thesis quality, examples used, time taken (aim under 30 min), and one thing to improve next time.
💼 Work Learning — Quick Captures
After any training or article: (1) what you learned, (2) how it connects to what you know, (3) one question it raised. Takes 5 minutes.
Practice Methods

How to practice — not just what to practice. Method is everything.

GRE Practice Methods
→
Timed sets, not individual questions. Always practice in timed sets of 10–20. Your brain needs to practice managing time and fatigue.
→
Brutal post-mortem every session. Spend as long reviewing wrong answers as solving problems. Understanding why you were wrong is 10x more valuable than doing more questions.
→
Simulate test conditions for mock tests. No phone. Timed. No pausing. Proper desk. Same time as your actual exam. Train for real conditions.
→
AWA: Write, then compare to scored samples. Write in 30 minutes, then read ETS scored samples at your target band. Fix one gap at a time.
→
Vocab: Spaced repetition only. Don't re-read lists. Use Anki or Magoosh. Reviewing words you already know is wasted time.
English Test Practice Methods
→
Speaking: Record yourself, then listen back. You'll hear filler words, grammar errors, and pacing issues you'd never notice otherwise.
→
Writing: Use actual prompts, timed. IELTS Task 1 = 20 min, Task 2 = 40 min. TOEFL Integrated = 20 min, Independent = 30 min. Time pressure is part of what you're training.
→
Listening: Active listening exercises. Pause a podcast after 2–3 minutes and write down the 3 main points from memory. This mimics TOEFL Integrated note-taking.
→
Immersion beats drills. A film in English for 2 hours is more effective for fluency than 2 hours of grammar exercises. Immersion makes the language feel natural under pressure.
